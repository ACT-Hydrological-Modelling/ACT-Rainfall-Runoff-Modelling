# Network calibration of rainfall-runoff models: simultaneous, sequential, or something in between

**Simultaneous whole-of-network calibration offers theoretical advantages in parameter consistency and routing-runoff coupling, but for a 50-subcatchment ACT network, it is almost certainly impractical without dimensionality reduction.** The peer-reviewed literature converges on a clear message: pure simultaneous calibration of independent parameter sets across dozens of subcatchments creates an optimization problem that overwhelms the information content of the available gauge data. The most robust path forward combines sequential calibration structure with regularization or parameter-sharing techniques that couple subcatchments without requiring full joint optimization. This report synthesizes findings from approximately 40 key studies spanning two decades of research on this question.

## The fundamental tension: information gain versus parameter explosion

The core dilemma in network calibration is well established. Each interior gauging station provides additional observational constraint, but each subcatchment with its own parameter set adds **10–16 free parameters** (Sacramento) or **4–5 parameters** (GR4J plus routing). For a 50-subcatchment ACT network using Sacramento, simultaneous calibration means searching a **500–750-dimensional space** — a regime where standard optimization algorithms struggle severely.

Doherty and Hunt (2009) demonstrated that a single streamflow time series can reliably identify only **3–8 parameters** of a watershed model, even when processed into multi-component objective functions. This finding has profound implications: with 50 gauges providing perhaps 400 identifiable parameter dimensions but 750 free parameters, the system remains fundamentally underdetermined. The landmark study by Lerat et al. (2012), testing four calibration strategies across **187 French catchments** using a semi-distributed rainfall-runoff model, delivered the sobering conclusion that **multi-site calibration did not outperform single-site calibration** — performance was equivalent at both outlet and interior points. The explanation is that when each subcatchment adds an independent parameter set, the additional observations from interior gauges fail to overcome the proportional increase in dimensionality.

This does not mean interior gauges are useless. Wi et al. (2015) found that pooled (simultaneous) multisite calibration **significantly outperformed** outlet-only calibration and also beat stepwise calibration at ungauged verification points in the Kabul River basin. The key distinction is that Wi et al. used semi-distributed parameterization (shared parameters across similar subcatchments), keeping effective dimensionality manageable. The literature is unambiguous on one point: **any form of multisite calibration far exceeds outlet-only calibration** for predicting flows at interior locations.

## What the head-to-head comparisons actually show

Three studies provide direct comparisons of simultaneous versus sequential calibration in network settings, and their results are notably mixed.

Ajami et al. (2004) compared lumped, semi-lumped, and semi-distributed calibration of the **Sacramento model** for the Illinois River basin under the DMIP project. The semi-lumped strategy — identical parameters across subcatchments with spatially varied precipitation — outperformed fully semi-distributed calibration with independent parameters per subcatchment. Varying parameters between sub-basins did not improve simulation at either the outlet or interior points. Khakbaz et al. (2012) extended this work to DMIP 2 and confirmed the finding. These results apply directly to the user's Sacramento implementation and suggest that parameter heterogeneity across subcatchments should be introduced cautiously.

Nkiaka et al. (2017) compared single-site, sequential, and simultaneous multi-site calibration of SWAT for the Logone catchment. **Sequential calibration outperformed both alternatives**, revealing differences in hydrological behavior between upstream and downstream areas that the simultaneous approach averaged away. However, Wi et al. (2015) found the opposite for HYMOD_DS: simultaneous pooled calibration outperformed stepwise calibration, particularly at verification gauges where stepwise showed downward bias and substantial performance variability. Zanin et al. (2018) found that simultaneous calibration produced **smaller uncertainty ranges** than sequential calibration in nested SWAT catchments.

The pattern that emerges is this: simultaneous calibration tends to win when parameterization is parsimonious (shared parameters, transfer functions), while sequential calibration tends to win when each subcatchment has fully independent parameters, because the sequential approach effectively decomposes the high-dimensional problem into tractable sub-problems.

## Multi-objective formulation: aggregation versus Pareto fronts

For network calibration with multiple gauges, the objective function formulation matters enormously. Two philosophies dominate the literature.

**Aggregated single-objective approaches** combine gauge-specific metrics (typically NSE or KGE) into a weighted sum and optimize using standard algorithms like SCE-UA or DREAM. Madsen (2003) demonstrated this for MIKE SHE with streamflow and groundwater observations, finding that a balanced weighted aggregation provided proper compromise between objectives. The approach is computationally simple and directly compatible with DREAM. However, Efstratiadis and Koutsoyiannis (2010) warned that aggregation **increases response surface complexity**, can cause optimizers to return dominated solutions, and destroys diagnostic information about inter-site trade-offs. Weight selection remains inherently subjective.

**Pareto-based multi-objective approaches** treat each gauge as a separate objective, using algorithms like NSGA-II, MOCOM-UA (Yapo et al., 1998), MOSCEM-UA (Vrugt et al., 2003), or AMALGAM (Vrugt and Robinson, 2007). These reveal trade-offs between sites — diagnostically valuable because asymmetric Pareto fronts indicate model structural deficiency. Zhang et al. (2010) found AMALGAM consistently outperformed NSGA-II and SPEA2 for multi-site SWAT calibration across four watersheds. However, Pareto-based methods face their own curse of dimensionality: Wu et al. (2024) tested calibration of **58 parameters against 24 objectives** and found that NSGA-II failed entirely, while DREAM provided better performance with informal likelihood functions but struggled with formal likelihoods. The practical ceiling appears to be **4–6 objectives** before Pareto methods lose effectiveness.

For the ACT network with potentially 50 gauges, neither pure approach scales well. The practical recommendation from the literature is a **hybrid strategy**: group gauges into 3–5 clusters (e.g., by major tributary), aggregate within clusters, and treat the cluster-level metrics as separate Pareto objectives. DREAM can handle this well when configured for multi-objective sampling. Alternatively, use an aggregated objective with DREAM for Bayesian posterior exploration, then diagnose site-specific performance post-hoc.

## Scalability hits a wall around 50–100 free parameters

The scalability evidence is sobering. Laloy and Vrugt (2012) developed MT-DREAM(ZS) specifically for high-dimensional problems and tested it up to **241 parameters**, but noted that "the current generation of search and optimization algorithms is typically not powerful enough to deal with a very large number of variables." The Sacramento model alone poses challenges for MCMC samplers due to "numerous local optima with both small and large domains of attraction, discontinuous first derivatives, and curving multidimensional ridges."

Wu et al. (2024) tested four calibration approaches including DREAM on 58 parameters against 24 objectives and concluded that **not a single parameter set could capture all objectives satisfactorily in 8 million model evaluations**. They recommended embracing equifinality and using ensembles of comparable parameter sets. For 500–750 parameters (50 Sacramento subcatchments), pure simultaneous calibration would require orders of magnitude more evaluations — likely billions — making it computationally prohibitive even with parallel computing.

Sequential calibration scales linearly: each sub-problem involves only **10–16 parameters** (Sacramento + routing) or **4–5 parameters** (GR4J + routing), well within DREAM's capability. The total computational cost is roughly N × cost-per-subcatchment rather than the exponentially growing cost of joint optimization. This is why continental-scale systems like the USGS NHM-PRMS and Australia's AWRA use staged sequential procedures.

## Hybrid strategies offer the best of both worlds

The most promising approaches occupy the space between pure sequential and pure simultaneous. Five distinct strategies emerge from the literature, ranked by relevance to the ACT application.

**Regularized sequential calibration** (de Lavenne et al., 2019) is the most directly applicable. Applied to semi-distributed GR4J networks, it modifies the standard upstream-to-downstream sequential procedure by adding a penalty term that penalizes parameter drift from a priori values. The objective function jointly maximizes simulation performance and minimizes deviation from prior parameter estimates. With a regularization weight k ≈ 0.15, performance at pseudo-ungauged subcatchments improved substantially. Standard sequential calibration fails to improve performance at ungauged interior points; regularization fixes this by enforcing spatial coherence. This approach is implemented in INRAE's airGRiwrm R package and could be straightforwardly adapted to the user's Python framework.

**Split-parameter structures** (Francés et al., 2007) offer dramatic dimensionality reduction. Each parameter is decomposed into a spatially variable a priori field (derived from soil, land-use, and topographic data) and a spatially uniform correction factor that is calibrated. For 50 subcatchments, instead of 750 free parameters, only **~15 correction factors** need calibration — one per parameter type. Francés et al. demonstrated this for 41 contiguous catchments in Spain using SCE-UA. Pokhrel et al. (2008, 2010) extended this with three "superparameters" per parameter field (adjusting mean, variance, and shape), offering more flexibility while keeping dimensionality around **30–45 parameters** for a full Sacramento implementation.

**Multiscale Parameter Regionalization** (MPR, Samaniego et al., 2010) uses transfer functions linking catchment attributes to model parameters. Only the transfer function coefficients are calibrated — typically **20–40 global parameters** regardless of network size. MPR is the backbone of the mesoscale Hydrologic Model (mHM) and has been applied across hundreds of catchments. Lane et al. (2021) extended it with uncertainty quantification for nationally consistent parameter fields. For the ACT, developing transfer functions from available soils, geology, and land-cover data would reduce the calibration problem to a tractable size while maintaining physical spatial variability.

**Hierarchical Bayesian approaches** (Kuczera, Kavetski, Thyer, Renard) treat subcatchment parameters as draws from a shared regional distribution, enabling partial pooling of information. This is conceptually elegant and well-suited to DREAM-based sampling, but the computational cost is high because latent variables (per-subcatchment parameters) must be sampled alongside hyperparameters. Deng et al. (2019) demonstrated reduced parameter uncertainty through spatial coherence constraints in this framework.

**Differentiable parameter learning** (Tsai et al., 2021; Feng et al., 2022) represents the newest frontier: neural networks map from catchment attributes to model parameters, and the entire system is trained end-to-end using gradient-based optimization. This achieves LSTM-level performance while maintaining physical interpretability. However, it requires substantial training data and a differentiable model implementation, which may not align with the user's existing codebase.

## Routing parameters interact strongly with rainfall-runoff calibration

Cortés-Salazar et al. (2023) provided definitive evidence that routing configuration **alters the optimal rainfall-runoff parameter space**. Testing 3,500 parameter sets with four routing schemes in a Chilean catchment, they found that including routing increased maximum KGE from **0.64 to 0.81** for daily streamflow, and that optimal parameter sets differed depending on the routing scheme used. This means routing and rainfall-runoff parameters are coupled — calibrating them in isolation risks parameter compensation.

Bindas et al. (2024) demonstrated this compensation mechanism directly: when routing parameters are constrained, bias from the rainfall-runoff component remains visible rather than being absorbed into distorted routing parameters. In sequential calibration, upstream rainfall-runoff errors can propagate and be partially absorbed by downstream routing parameters, creating compensating biases that degrade physical interpretability even when aggregate performance metrics look acceptable.

**Simultaneous calibration of routing and rainfall-runoff parameters handles this coupling better** in principle, but only if the dimensionality is manageable. For the ACT network, the practical recommendation is to calibrate Muskingum or lag-route parameters jointly with rainfall-runoff parameters within each sequential step, rather than in a separate stage. This captures local routing-runoff coupling without requiring full network-wide simultaneous optimization. The de Lavenne regularization approach can be extended to include routing parameter regularization.

## Practical path forward for a 50-subcatchment ACT network

The literature points toward a clear practical strategy for the user's specific situation, synthesized from the consensus across studies:

- **Do not attempt pure simultaneous calibration** of 500–750 independent parameters. The optimization problem is almost certainly ill-conditioned, DREAM will not converge reliably in this space, and the results are unlikely to outperform simpler approaches. Lerat et al. (2012) and Ajami et al. (2004) both found that independent per-subcatchment parameters did not improve performance over more parsimonious configurations.

- **GR4J has a significant advantage over Sacramento** for network calibration due to its parsimony — 4 parameters versus 13–16. A 50-subcatchment GR4J network requires ~200–250 parameters versus ~650–800 for Sacramento, a difference that matters enormously for identifiability. If both models are being considered, GR4J is substantially more tractable for network-scale calibration.

- **The strongest approach is regularized sequential calibration with parameter sharing.** Calibrate upstream to downstream using DREAM, with each step jointly optimizing the local subcatchment's rainfall-runoff and routing parameters. Add a regularization penalty (following de Lavenne et al., 2019) that constrains parameters toward either upstream-calibrated values or physically-derived a priori estimates. This keeps each sub-problem at 4–16 dimensions, well within DREAM's sweet spot.

- **Consider a split-parameter or transfer-function structure** to reduce dimensionality further. If ACT subcatchments share similar geology and land cover, a split-parameter approach (Francés et al., 2007) could reduce the effective calibration to ~15–45 global parameters, making a single simultaneous calibration with DREAM entirely feasible. This is where simultaneous calibration becomes practical: not by calibrating 750 independent parameters, but by calibrating 15–45 transfer function coefficients that determine all spatial parameters through physical relationships.

- **Australia's eWater Source platform** already supports both sequential and simultaneous multi-gauge calibration with parameter grouping, and the AWRA modelling system uses staged sequential coupling (AWRA-L landscape model → AWRA-R river model). The user's Python implementation can draw on these established Australian frameworks for design guidance.

## Conclusion

The literature does not support a binary choice between simultaneous and sequential calibration. Rather, it reveals that **dimensionality management is the master variable**. When effective parameter dimensionality is kept tractable — through transfer functions, split-parameter structures, regularization, or hierarchical priors — simultaneous calibration produces more robust, spatially coherent parameters with smaller uncertainty bounds. When each subcatchment retains fully independent parameters, sequential calibration is not only more practical but often performs equally well or better, because the information content of interior gauges cannot overcome the proportional dimensionality increase.

For the ACT's 50-subcatchment network, the highest-value next step is investing in physically-derived a priori parameter fields from available soils, geology, and land-cover data, then calibrating a small set of global correction factors or transfer function coefficients simultaneously against all gauges using DREAM. This collapses a 750-dimensional nightmare into a 15–45-dimensional problem that DREAM can solve confidently, while preserving the spatial heterogeneity that makes distributed modelling worthwhile. If a priori parameter derivation is impractical, regularized sequential calibration with DREAM offers a robust, well-tested fallback that is straightforward to implement in the existing Python framework.
